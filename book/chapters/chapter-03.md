# Chapter 3: Apps and AI Copilots: Choosing the Right Tool

Elena Fuentes manages the campus bookstore, and her Monday starts with a mess that has nothing to do with books. The fall sales report is due to the college's business office, and last year's version was a plain wall of text that took her predecessor two days to format by hand. Meanwhile her laptop offers her six different ways to write it, her browser offers six more, and an AI assistant volunteers to draft the whole thing. None of those tools is the problem. The problem is choosing, and choosing is a skill.

This chapter builds that skill. Chapters 1 and 2 handed you tools one at a time: Word for a letter, a browser for verification, a memo for findings. Now you will step back and look at the whole toolbox. Application software comes in families, each built for a kind of task, and the working question is never "what is the best app?" It is "what does this task need?" People who can answer that question quickly are the ones everyone else asks for help, in every field this course serves.

Three moves this chapter, then a payoff. First, you will sort the everyday applications and the specialized ones by the tasks they serve. Second, you will see where apps live now (installed, in the browser, and on your phone) and how suites, subscriptions, and app stores change what you pay and what you get. Third, you will meet the AI copilots riding inside the apps and draw the line between what they draft and what you decide. The payoff is the Skills Lab, where Word's styles turn Elena's wall of text into a report that formats itself, table of contents included.

## 3.1 The Everyday Four

Chapter 1 sorted software into two layers and put four file families on your map: documents, worksheets, presentations, and databases. Behind those four families stand four kinds of **general-purpose applications**, programs built for broad tasks that nearly every job needs. Offices run on them, which is why this course spends twelve labs inside them.

### What Each One Is For

A **word processor** (Word) builds documents: text that flows in paragraphs and pages. Its deep skill is structure and polish: styles, tables of contents, headers, and the editing tools you used in Chapters 1 and 2. If the output will be read as pages, the word processor is home.

A **spreadsheet program** (Excel) builds worksheets: numbers in a grid that recalculate themselves. Change one price and every total that depends on it updates instantly. If the output is calculation, comparison, or a chart, the grid wins, and Chapters 6 through 9 live there.

**Presentation software** (PowerPoint) builds slides that support a talk. Its skill is pacing ideas for an audience: one point at a time, backed by visuals. If the output is a room of people following you, slides are the tool, and Chapters 4 and 5 build them.

A **database manager** (Airtable in this course) builds databases: organized records you can search, filter, and connect. When the questions sound like "which customers ordered twice this month?", a database answers them without breaking a sweat. Chapters 10 and 11 build one.

| Application | Builds | Reach for it when |
| ----------- | ------ | ----------------- |
| Word processor | Documents | The output is read as pages |
| Spreadsheet program | Worksheets | The output is calculation or a chart |
| Presentation software | Slide decks | The output is a talk with an audience |
| Database manager | Databases | The output is answers from many records |

Two honorary members round out the everyday set: the browser and email, both toured in Chapter 2. They are general-purpose applications too, but they carry work instead of building files, which is why this chapter centers on the four builders.

### The Overlap Trap

Here is the catch: each of the four can fake the others' jobs, badly. Word can hold a table of numbers, but the numbers just sit there. Excel can hold paragraphs, one cramped cell at a time. PowerPoint can hold a report nobody can read from row three. The wrong tool rarely refuses the work. It just makes every later step slower, and the cost lands weeks after the choice.

The bookstore's old inventory "system" makes the point. Somebody once listed 400 textbook titles in a Word document because Word was open at the time. Every reorder meant scrolling, retyping, and praying. The same 400 titles in a spreadsheet sort by course in one click. In a database, they connect to suppliers and sales history. Same data, three tools, three different weeks for whoever inherits it. Tool choice is a gift, or a debt, to your future self and coworkers.

### Better Together

The four are not rivals. They are a relay team, and professional work usually passes a baton. Elena's fall report is a Word document, but its sales chart will be built in Excel, where the numbers live, and pasted into the page. When she presents the results to the student senate, the report's headings become the outline of a PowerPoint deck. When the business office asks "which departments ordered the most spirit wear," that answer comes from records, not paragraphs.

The pattern to learn: keep each kind of content in its home tool, then hand results across. Numbers live in the grid and visit the document. Structure lives in the document and seeds the deck. Records live in the database and feed everything. You will run this relay yourself across the course: the Chapter 6 workbook feeds the Chapter 9 analysis, and the Chapter 10 base answers questions no worksheet could hold.

## 3.2 Specialized Tools for Specialized Work

The everyday four are generalists. Around them stands a second family: **specialized applications**, programs built deep for one kind of work. No office runs on them alone, but most careers touch at least one, and knowing they exist keeps you from forcing the everyday four into jobs they were never built for.

### Making Media: Graphics, Video, and Audio

Graphics software edits and creates images, and it splits along a line worth knowing. A **raster image** is a grid of colored pixels, which is what cameras produce and photo editors edit. A **vector image** is built from shapes and math, so logos and icons scale to any size without blurring. That is why the bookstore's logo lives as a vector file and its storefront photos live as raster files, and why editing each takes a different tool.

Video editing software assembles clips, sound, and titles into finished video, the format the creator economy from Chapter 2 runs on. Audio tools record and polish sound for podcasts, music, and voiceover. All of these now ship with AI features (background removal, auto-captions, noise cleanup) that compress hours of skilled labor into a click. The verification catch travels with them: the click is fast, and judging whether the result is right stays your job.

### Building Sites and Games

Web authoring tools build websites without hand-writing the HTML you met in Chapter 2. Modern site builders let a shop like Desert Bloom assemble a storefront from **templates**, prebuilt designs you fill with your own content, which is exactly how Maya's bean store will likely ship. The HTML is still there underneath. The tool writes it for you, and now you know what it is writing.

Game development runs on a **game engine**, software that supplies a game's physics, graphics, and logic so creators build the game instead of the plumbing. Game design looks like play from the outside, but inside it is one of the most tool-heavy specialties in software, blending code, art, audio, and testing.

### The Long Tail of Specialties

The specialized family runs a long tail past media and games. Accounting software closes the bookstore's books. Project management tools track who owes what by when. Architects and engineers draft in CAD software, researchers test results in statistical packages, and clinics chart in electronic health records. You will not learn these tools here, and you do not need to. You need the reflex of asking whether a specialized tool exists before bending a general one, and the five-question habit in Section 3.4 for deciding when the specialist earns its cost.

!!! tip "Tech in Your Field"
    Specialized applications are where majors meet their daily tools. Nursing and health sciences students will work inside electronic health record systems, specialized database applications where accuracy is patient safety. Business and entrepreneurship students will run point-of-sale and accounting software that feeds the dashboards Chapter 9 examines. Visual and performing arts students will build careers inside graphics, video, and audio tools, plus the platforms that distribute the work. Public safety and law students will file into records-management and dispatch systems built for evidence-grade accuracy. In every case the specialized tool changed the job, and the professional still signs the work. The generalist skills in this course are what let you learn each new tool faster than the person next to you.

## 3.3 Where Apps Live: Installed, Web, and Mobile

The same application now reaches you three ways, and the choice among them is part of tool selection. Word is the cleanest example, because you will meet all three of its forms in this course.

### Three Homes, One App

An **installed application** is set up on the device itself. It is the full-power form: every feature, works offline, and handles big files best. The Word you used in Skills Lab 1A is this form, and the macro work in Chapter 12 requires it, because some deep features live only in installed apps.

A **web app** runs in the browser with nothing to install: sign in and work. Web apps are always current, follow you to any machine, and make sharing one document with three coworkers painless. The trade: they need a connection, and they usually carry fewer features than their installed twins. Excel for the web, for example, cannot record or run the macros Chapter 12 teaches. Airtable, this course's database manager, is a web app by design.

A **mobile app** is built for phones and tablets: touch first, camera and sensors built in, notifications on your body. Mobile apps win for capture (photograph the receipt, scan the barcode, dictate the note) and for anything done standing up. Long-form building, like Elena's report, punishes a phone keyboard.

| | Installed | Web | Mobile |
| --- | --------- | --- | ------ |
| Strengths | Every feature, works offline, big files | Always current, any machine, easy co-editing | Capture, sensors, notifications, always with you |
| Limits | Tied to one device, needs updates | Needs a connection, fewer features | Small screen, slow for long-form work |
| In this course | Skills Labs, and required for Chapter 12's macros | Airtable (Chapters 10-11), quick edits and sharing | Capturing and checking, not building |

The practical pattern for one person's work, and the reason Microsoft 365 gives you all three forms: capture on mobile, polish installed, share and co-edit on the web.

The homes also decide where your file lives, which Chapter 1's saving habits already touched. Installed apps default to files on the device (or in the cloud when AutoSave is on). Web apps keep the file in the cloud from birth, which is what makes three-person co-editing painless: everyone edits the one copy, instead of emailing `report-v3-final.docx` around until nobody knows which copy is true. When collaborators enter the picture, a shared cloud file beats attachments every time.

### Suites, Subscriptions, and App Stores

Applications also come bundled and billed in ways that shape what you get. A **software suite** is a set of applications sold together and built to work together: Microsoft 365 bundles Word, Excel, PowerPoint, and more, with shared formats and one sign-in. Suites are why your Chapter 1 letter, Chapter 2 memo, and Chapter 6 workbook will all feel related.

A **subscription** bills for software monthly or yearly instead of one purchase, and it now dominates: continuous updates and cloud storage in exchange for a fee that never ends. (Your Microsoft 365 subscription is free through the college while you are enrolled, which is the version this course targets.) The alternative one-time purchase still exists, frozen at the features it shipped with. Free plans, like Airtable's, offer a working slice of a paid product, and this course stays inside them on purpose.

An **app store** is a curated marketplace for software: search, reviews, one-tap install, automatic updates, and a vetting layer that screens (imperfectly) for malware. Stores made installing software safe enough for everyone, in exchange for fees and rules that developers grumble about. When you install from the open web instead, Chapter 8's caution applies: you become the vetting layer.

Two more facts round out the buying picture. First, suites compete, and file formats mostly cooperate. The `.docx` you produce in Word opens in rival suites (Google's, and free ones like LibreOffice), though complex formatting can shift in translation. The PDF habit from Chapter 1 still guards anything that must look right. Second, "free" software divides into honestly free (open source, like three of this chapter's Further Reading tools), free plans that sell upgrades, and free products that sell your attention or your data. Chapter 8 examines that last price tag closely.

!!! note
    Before paying for any software as a student, check what your college already provides. Your enrollment includes Microsoft 365, and many specialized tools (statistics packages, design apps, cloud credits) carry free education plans. The five-question habit starts with what you already have.

## 3.4 Copilots and the Decision Habit

One more resident has moved into every home the apps live in. This section names it, draws the line around it, and then hands you the decision habit the whole chapter has been building toward.

### The Copilot Moves In

An **AI copilot** is an AI assistant built into an application, aware of the document, worksheet, or deck you are working on. Where the chatbots and answer engines of Chapter 2 live outside your files, a copilot reads the report you are writing and offers to extend it. (A **chatbot** is any software you converse with in plain language. Copilot, capitalized, is Microsoft's product name for the copilots inside Microsoft 365.) Inside Word a copilot drafts and rewrites. Inside Excel it suggests formulas and summarizes tables. Inside PowerPoint it roughs out slides from a document, and inside email it drafts replies in seconds.

The same copilot pattern is settling into every general-purpose app:

| Where it lives | What it drafts | What you verify before trusting it |
| -------------- | -------------- | ---------------------------------- |
| Word processor | Paragraphs, rewrites, summaries | Facts, names, numbers, and tone |
| Spreadsheet program | Formulas and table summaries | The logic, against numbers you know |
| Presentation software | Slide outlines from a document | Emphasis: what it promoted and what it dropped |
| Email | Replies and thread summaries | Commitments made in your name |

Some copilot features require paid plans, which is why no lab in this course requires one. The skill transfers anyway: this course teaches you to direct and judge AI drafting using the free tools you already have, and the judging works the same whatever the tool costs.

### What It Drafts, What You Decide

Chapter 1 gave you the split, and it holds under every new feature name: assistants draft, people decide. The copilot's failures are quieter than the chatbot's, which makes verification more important, not less. A drafted paragraph can be fluent and subtly off-message. A suggested formula can be plausible and wrong, and a wrong formula in a worksheet fails silently, which is why Chapter 12 teaches you to read generated code before trusting it. A summarized document can drop the one sentence a decision hinged on.

So the professional pattern inside any app is the same one you ran in the Chapter 2 lab:

1. **Direct.** Give the copilot context and a specific ask, the way you would brief an intern.
2. **Draft.** Let it produce the fast first version.
3. **Verify.** Check names, numbers, claims, and tone against sources you trust, including your own knowledge of the situation.
4. **Decide and sign.** Edit until you would stand behind every sentence. The signature line from Chapter 1 has not moved.

In practice the exchange looks like this:

```text
You:      Draft a three-sentence summary of this fall report
          for the business office. Formal, no superlatives.
Copilot:  (a fluent draft appears in seconds)
You:      Check each number against the report's own tables,
          cut the claim the data does not support, sign it.
```

The first and last lines are the job. The middle line is the tool.

### When the Answer Is No AI

Question five of the decision habit sometimes returns a hard no, and knowing those cases is part of the skill. Three show up early in working life:

* **Confidential content.** Whatever you paste into an AI tool travels to that tool's servers. Customer lists, patient details, unreleased numbers, and other people's private messages do not belong in a free chatbot, and many employers now say so in writing. When in doubt, ask before you paste.
* **Work that must be yours.** Courses, licensure exams, and some workplaces require your own unassisted work, and the rules differ by class and employer. Read the policy, follow it, and when AI use is allowed, disclose it honestly. Chapter 8 takes up AI disclosure as an ethics topic in its own right.
* **Judgment you were hired for.** Recommending which employee to promote, which patient concern to escalate, which supplier to drop: a copilot will draft any of these fluently. Delegating the decision, not just the drafting, is how professionals quietly stop being necessary.

None of this is fear of the tools. It is the same boundary this book drew in Chapter 1, applied to specific stakes: the tools draft, you decide, and some decisions should not even be drafted.

### Task First, Software Second

Now assemble the whole chapter into one habit. Before opening any app, answer five questions, in order:

1. **What is the deliverable?** Pages, a grid, slides, records, or media. This names the application family (Sections 3.1 and 3.2).
2. **Who is it for, and how will they use it?** A reader, an audience, a searcher, a grader. This sets the quality bar and the format.
3. **Where will I work on it?** At a desk, on the move, with collaborators. This picks installed, web, or mobile (Section 3.3).
4. **What do I already have?** The suite you own (or your school provides) beats a new tool that duplicates it. New tools cost money, learning time, and one more login.
5. **Where does AI help, and where does it risk?** Drafting and summarizing, yes, with verification. Final judgment, numbers you have not checked, and anything you must stand behind: yours.

Run Elena's report through the five. The deliverable is pages for the business office: Word. The readers are busy administrators: styled and professional. The work happens at a desk: installed. The college already provides the suite: no new tool. And AI may suggest phrasing, but it does not get to invent sales numbers.

The habit also protects you from its opposite, the tool hop. Every field has someone who moves the team to a new app every month, and every move costs setup, relearning, and the old tool's stranded files. New tools earn a switch by answering the five questions better than what you have, not by being new. The boring answer that ships the work usually beats the exciting answer that ships a migration. Five questions, one defensible choice, and that is the Skills Lab you are about to do. Task first, software second is also how this book proceeds from here: every remaining lab states the task, and by Chapter 11 choosing the tool stack will be the assignment itself.

## 3.5 Summary and Retrieval 💡

### Key Concepts

* Four general-purpose applications carry office work: word processors build documents, spreadsheet programs build recalculating worksheets, presentation software builds slide decks, and database managers build searchable records. Each can fake the others' jobs badly, and the cost of the wrong tool lands later, on you or a coworker.
* Specialized applications go deep on one kind of work: raster and vector graphics, video and audio, web authoring that writes Chapter 2's HTML for you, and game engines. Most careers touch at least one.
* The same app now lives three ways: installed (full power, offline, required for macros), web (current, shareable, connection required), and mobile (capture and quick action). Suites bundle apps that work together, subscriptions bill continuously for continuous updates, and app stores add a vetting layer to installation.
* An AI copilot is an assistant inside the app, aware of your document. It drafts fast and fails quietly, so the pattern is direct, draft, verify, decide, and the signature stays yours.
* Task first, software second: deliverable, audience, workplace, what you already have, and where AI helps or risks. Five questions, answered in order, beat every "best app" listicle ever written.
* Word styles label a document's structure the way HTML tags label a page, and labeled structure is what lets the document build its own table of contents.

## 3.6 Skills Lab 3A: Reports That Build Themselves: Styles and a Table of Contents

**Goal:** Turn the campus bookstore's plain-text fall report into a styled, professionally structured Word document with a self-updating table of contents, ready for the college's business office.

**Dataset or starter files:** One provided file in `assets/code/chapter-03/` from the course data pack. `bookstore-report.docx` is Elena's complete report text, correct in content and bare of all formatting: five section titles in capital letters, three subsection titles marked with a leading `>>`, and body paragraphs. Download the data pack from Canvas, extract it, and work at the extracted `cis105` root. You will restructure the provided text, never retype it.

**Why styles first:** A **style** is a named set of formatting (Heading 1, Heading 2, Title, Normal) that you apply to a paragraph in one click. Styles do more than format. They label structure, the way Chapter 2's HTML tags label a page, and Word can read those labels. The table of contents builds itself from your headings. Changing a style's look updates every heading that wears it. Direct formatting (selecting a line and making it big and bold by hand) looks identical and labels nothing. This lab is the difference, felt.

### Part 1: Foundation (Aligns with MLO-3.3)

1. Open `bookstore-report.docx` from the data pack. Before touching anything, save your own working copy with File > Save As (or Save a Copy), named `skills-lab-3a-lastname.docx`, so the original stays clean in the pack.
2. Apply the Title style (Home > Styles gallery) to the report's first line, and the Subtitle style to the second line (the semester and author line).
3. Find the five section titles written in capital letters. Apply Heading 1 to each. Then fix the capitalization without retyping: select each title and use Home > Change Case (the `Aa` button) > Capitalize Each Word, lowering small words like "and" and "for" by hand.
4. Find the three lines marked with `>>` inside the Sales by Category section. Delete the `>>` markers and apply Heading 2 to each.
5. Scroll the document once. Structure should now be visible at a glance. Then open View > Navigation Pane (View > Sidebar > Navigation on a Mac) and watch your headings double as a clickable outline.

### Part 2: Application (Aligns with MLO-3.3)

1. Put your cursor at the start of the first section heading and insert a page break (Insert > Page Break), so the report body starts on its own page below the title block.
2. On the title page, below the subtitle, insert a table of contents: References > Table of Contents, any automatic style. Watch it assemble itself from your headings, page numbers included.
3. Add page numbers in the footer (Insert > Page Number) and run Editor once, the Chapter 1 habit.
4. Now collect the payoff. Modify the Heading 1 style once (right-click Heading 1 in the Styles gallery > Modify, change the color to a professional dark blue): every section heading updates together. Direct formatting would have made you do that five times, and miss one.

### Part 3: Extension (Aligns with MLO-3.1, MLO-3.2)

1. Elena wants Recommendations for Spring where the business office will see it early. Move that entire section (heading and body) to sit right after Overview. Select it in one sweep, cut, and paste, or drag its heading in the Navigation Pane, which moves the whole section in one motion.
2. The table of contents is now wrong, and this is the moment the lab exists for. Click the table and choose Update Table > Update entire table. One click, correct again. Write one sentence, as a comment attached to the table of contents (Review > New Comment), telling Elena how to update it next semester.
3. Add a second comment on the Recommendations for Spring heading. In two or three sentences, advise Elena whether an AI copilot should draft next semester's first version of this report, and what she must verify if it does. Ground your advice in Section 3.4's pattern.
4. Run one full pass of Chapter 1's pre-send inspection (names, dates, one font family, Editor, read-aloud), then export `skills-lab-3a-lastname.pdf`. Export before adding the Questions & Analysis page, so the PDF stays a clean deliverable.
