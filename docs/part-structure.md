# CIS105 Textbook: 12-Chapter Structure (Authoritative)

**Course:** CIS105: Survey of Computer Information Systems
**Structure:** 12 chapters organized into 4 thematic Parts
**Schedule:** 12 content weeks + 1 review/exam week + 1 finals week
= 14-week term (a 16-week section adds a project week after Part III
and a buffer week before finals)
**Prerequisite:** None. Chapters teach from zero.
**Last revised:** 2026-07-11 (retitled and reframed for 2026)

This document is the single authoritative plan for chapter order,
scope, titles, and outcome coverage.

---

## Design Rationale

This plan synthesizes three sources:

1. **Official district CLOs and course outline**
   (docs/CIS105_CLOs.md): every outcome and outline section maps to a
   chapter
2. **The previous Canvas build:** 13 modules following O'Leary's
   *Computing Essentials 2023* (29e) with SIMnet labs. The proven
   topic ORDER carries forward. The titles, framing, and examples do
   not.
3. **The textbook family structure:** 12 chapters in 4 Parts, the
   pattern proven by the sibling textbooks

**Key design decisions:**

* **Title freshness rule (Mr. Vega, 2026-07-11):** no part name,
  chapter title, section heading, or assignment title reproduces a
  heading from the old notes or the old SIMnet units. The complete
  banned list, with a grep check, lives in
  docs/legacy-title-blocklist.md. New titles may share common words
  with old ones. They may not match exactly.
* **2026 reframe:** this book teaches the district outline as it
  exists in students' 2026 lives. AI assistants sit inside every
  productivity app, answer engines sit beside search engines, passkeys
  are replacing passwords, laptops ship with neural processors, and
  every major in the room (not just BAS-IT) works differently because
  of it. Each chapter carries at least one AI-era thread, and the
  through-line of the whole book is: your role is changing, not
  disappearing, and the judgment stays with you.
* **Audience (Mr. Vega, 2026-07-11):** CIS105 serves every major.
  Typical enrollment runs about half Business and Entrepreneurship,
  about a quarter BAS-IT, and the rest across Biosciences,
  Communication and World Languages, Counseling, Health Sciences,
  Liberal Arts, Mathematics, Nursing, Public Safety and Law, and
  Visual and Performing Arts. Every chapter includes one
  **Tech in Your Field** admonition connecting the topic to at least
  three of those majors (format in CLAUDE.md).
* **Proven order kept.** Old modules 6 (input/output) and 7 (storage)
  merge into Chapter 6. Every other module keeps its taught order, so
  the coverage matrices below remain valid.
* **Airtable replaces Microsoft Access** (Mr. Vega's standing
  decision, recorded twice in the source outline).
* **Skills Lab app thread** covers CLO XIII: Word (Ch 1-3),
  PowerPoint (Ch 4-5), Excel (Ch 6-9), Airtable (Ch 10-11), Excel
  macros with VBA (Ch 12). Labs use authentic tasks instead of
  simulations, and each lab now carries its own title (listed per
  chapter below).
* **VBA and macros live in Chapter 12** with programming concepts
  (CLO VII, outline section V), now paired with AI coding assistants:
  students record a macro, read the generated code, and verify code
  they did not write. That is the 2026 programming skill.
* **Privacy, security, and ethics stay one chapter (Ch 8)** paired
  with networks in Part III, so risk always follows connectivity.

**Bloom's rule for every MLO (course-configured, mirrors CLAUDE.md):**
the full RBT range is available. Early chapters lean on Remember
through Apply verbs. Analyze through Create verbs appear in Skills
Labs, later chapters, and the VBA work in Chapter 12. Every MLO uses a
measurable action verb. Never write "understand," "know," or "learn"
as the verb.

## Part I: Your Digital Toolkit (Chapters 1-3)

**Theme:** the system you already live inside, and the tools that
changed every job
**Learning arc:** Describe the system -> Work the web -> Choose the tool
**Outcome alignment:** CLO II, IV, V, X (CLO XIII lab thread: Word)
**Bloom's focus:** Remember, Understand, Apply

### Chapter 1: People, Data, and Intelligent Machines

**Subtitle:** DESCRIBE the parts that turn data into useful work in
any career
**Outcome(s):** CLO X (primary), CLO I, II, V (introduced)
**Outline section(s):** VIII (uses of technology), survey of I-IV

**Content:**

* The six moving parts behind every app: people, procedures, software,
  hardware, data, and connectivity
* Why people still lead the system: judgment, verification, and
  deciding when to trust an answer
* System software and application software at a glance
* Computers you will meet this term: phones, laptops, AI PCs, servers,
  and wearables
* Files you will produce all term: documents, worksheets, databases,
  and presentations
* The course roadmap: cloud computing, the Internet of Things, and the
  AI assistants now built into everyday tools
* How AI is reshaping (not erasing) the work every major trains for

**Prior-course parallel:** old module 1 (I.T., the Internet, and You)
**Prerequisite bridge:** new content. Bridges from students' daily
phone and app experience.
**Skills Lab 1 (Word):** From Blank Page to Business Letter. Draft,
edit, and deliver an announcement letter for the Phoenix-area coffee
shop.

### Chapter 2: The Web at Work: Search, Communication, and E-commerce

**Subtitle:** USE the web to find, verify, communicate, and buy
**Outcome(s):** CLO IV, V (primary), CLO VI, XI (introduced)
**Outline section(s):** IV (Internet)

**Content:**

* How the Internet grew from a research project into a public utility
* What happens when you type an address: browsers, web servers, and
  HTML
* Search engines and AI answer engines: getting results versus getting
  the truth
* Evaluating information when anyone (or any model) can publish
* Email, messaging, video calls, and professional communication norms
* Social platforms, podcasts, and the creator economy
* E-commerce: storefronts, marketplaces, and safe transactions

**Prior-course parallel:** old module 2 (Internet, Web, and Electronic
Commerce)
**Prerequisite bridge:** extends Chapter 1's connectivity roadmap.
**Skills Lab 2 (Word):** Fact-Check the Machine: A Research Memo.
Format a memo that verifies AI-generated claims (shipped in the data
pack) against credible sources.

### Chapter 3: Apps and AI Copilots: Choosing the Right Tool

**Subtitle:** CHOOSE the right application and decide when AI belongs
in the loop
**Outcome(s):** CLO II (primary), CLO IX (introduced), CLO XIII
**Outline section(s):** II.B-D (application software), IX (business
application tools, concepts)

**Content:**

* General-purpose applications: word processors, spreadsheets,
  presentation software, and database managers
* Specialized applications: graphics, video, audio, web authoring, and
  game design
* Apps on every screen: installed, web, and mobile, and when each wins
* Software suites, subscriptions, and app stores
* AI copilots inside everyday apps: what they draft, what you still
  decide
* Task first, software second: a decision habit for picking tools

**Prior-course parallel:** old module 3 (Application Software)
**Prerequisite bridge:** names the categories behind the tools used in
Chapters 1-2 labs.
**Skills Lab 3 (Word):** Reports That Build Themselves: Styles and a
Table of Contents. Turn a campus bookstore report into a styled,
self-updating document and share it for review.

**Part I milestone:** students can explain how people, data, and
machines work together, verify what they find online, and produce
polished Word documents.

## Part II: Under the Hood (Chapters 4-6)

**Theme:** what makes the machine run, from the OS to the chips to
every port and drive
**Learning arc:** Explain the software layer -> Identify the components -> Select devices and storage
**Outcome alignment:** CLO I, II (CLO XIII lab thread: PowerPoint, Excel)
**Bloom's focus:** Understand, Apply, Analyze

### Chapter 4: What Makes Your Device Run: Operating Systems and Utilities

**Subtitle:** EXPLAIN what the operating system does for you
**Outcome(s):** CLO II (primary)
**Outline section(s):** II.A (system software)

**Content:**

* The software layer you never see: booting, managing resources, and
  running apps
* Operating system functions, features, and user interfaces
* Mobile platforms: iOS and Android, and the assistants built into
  them
* Desktop platforms: Windows, macOS, Linux, and virtualization
* Cloud desktops: why the OS is quietly moving online
* Utilities, device drivers, and updates: why patching keeps you safe
* OS-level AI assistants and what they can see (a privacy preview of
  Chapter 8)

**Prior-course parallel:** old module 4 (System Software)
**Prerequisite bridge:** builds on the software split introduced in
Chapters 1 and 3.
**Skills Lab 4 (PowerPoint):** Pitch Deck Foundations: An Outline
Becomes Slides. Build the event venue's pitch presentation from a
provided outline.

### Chapter 5: Processors, Memory, and the AI PC

**Subtitle:** IDENTIFY the components that decide what a computer can
do
**Outcome(s):** CLO I (primary), CLO VIII (purchasing decisions)
**Outline section(s):** I.A, I.E (processing, expansion), VI.B
(purchasing and upgrading)

**Content:**

* Desktops, laptops, tablets, and wearables: same parts, different
  packages
* System boards, sockets, slots, and bus lines
* CPUs, GPUs, and NPUs: what makes a computer an "AI PC"
* How memory works: RAM, ROM, flash, and unified memory
* Ports, expansion cards, and power
* Reading a spec sheet: buying and upgrading without the jargon

**Prior-course parallel:** old module 5 (The System Unit)
**Prerequisite bridge:** hardware overview from Chapter 1 becomes
component-level detail.
**Skills Lab 5 (PowerPoint):** Show, Don't Tell: Hardware Specs as
Visuals. Turn the device repair shop's spec sheets into an
information-graphic deck.

### Chapter 6: From Touchscreens to the Cloud: Devices and Storage

**Subtitle:** SELECT the devices and storage that fit the task
**Outcome(s):** CLO I (primary)
**Outline section(s):** I.B-D (storage, input, output devices), VII.D
(ergonomics)

**Content:**

* Input today: touch, voice, cameras, scanners, sensors, and game
  controllers
* Output today: displays, printers, audio, and AR/VR headsets
* Ergonomics: setting up a workspace your body can live with
* Where data waits: memory versus storage
* Solid-state drives, hard drives, optical media, and memory cards
* Cloud storage and sync: your files on every device
* Backup habits: the 3-2-1 rule, before you need it

**Prior-course parallel:** old modules 6 (input/output) and 7
(storage), merged
**Prerequisite bridge:** extends Chapter 5's memory discussion into
persistent storage.
**Skills Lab 6 (Excel):** Side by Side: A Device Comparison Workbook.
Build the repair shop's device comparison sheet from pack data.

**Part II milestone:** students can trace what happens inside the
machine, compare hardware options against a task, and build both a
presentation and a working Excel workbook.

## Part III: Connected and Protected (Chapters 7-8)

**Theme:** how data travels, and how to defend it on the way
**Learning arc:** Explain connectivity -> Examine the risks -> Judge safe and ethical practice
**Outcome alignment:** CLO III, VI, XI (CLO XIII lab thread: Excel)
**Bloom's focus:** Understand, Analyze, Evaluate

### Chapter 7: Networks Everywhere: From Wi-Fi to Satellite Internet

**Subtitle:** EXPLAIN how data travels between people, devices, and
the cloud
**Outcome(s):** CLO III (primary), CLO VI (network security intro)
**Outline section(s):** III (networks)

**Content:**

* Connectivity concepts and communication systems
* Physical and wireless channels: fiber, cellular and 5G, Wi-Fi,
  Bluetooth, and satellite constellations
* Connection services: choosing a provider without the sales pitch
* Bandwidth and protocols: what the speed numbers mean
* Network types and scales: PAN, LAN, home networks, MAN, WAN
* Client/server, peer-to-peer, and shared resources
* Smart homes, IoT fleets, and computing at the edge

**Prior-course parallel:** old module 8 (Communications and Networks)
**Prerequisite bridge:** the connectivity thread from Chapters 1-2
becomes technical.
**Skills Lab 7 (Excel):** Let Formulas Do the Math: Choosing an
Internet Plan. Compare providers with formulas and functions.

### Chapter 8: Digital Self-Defense: Security, Privacy, and Ethics in the AI Era

**Subtitle:** JUDGE the risks of a connected life and choose the
safeguards that fit
**Outcome(s):** CLO VI, XI (primary)
**Outline section(s):** VII (social and ethical issues), III.C, IV.H
(security)

**Content:**

* Your data trail: large databases, data brokers, and online identity
* Major privacy laws and the rights they give you
* Cybercrime now: AI-written phishing, deepfakes, voice-clone scams,
  ransomware, and identity theft
* How attacks start: social engineering and malware
* Protecting yourself: passkeys, multifactor authentication,
  encryption, updates, and backups
* Ethics: copyright, plagiarism, AI disclosure, and cyberbullying
* Sustainable computing: device lifecycles and the energy cost of AI

**Prior-course parallel:** old module 9 (Privacy, Security, and Ethics)
**Prerequisite bridge:** every threat maps to a network path from
Chapter 7.
**Skills Lab 8 (Excel):** Score Your Own Security: An Audit in Charts.
Chart a personal security audit and defend the priorities it reveals.

**Part III milestone:** students can map how their data travels, name
the threats along the way (including the AI-powered ones), and defend
their choices with evidence and ethical reasoning.

## Part IV: Data, Decisions, and Automation (Chapters 9-12)

**Theme:** how organizations turn data into decisions, and how you
turn repetitive work into code
**Learning arc:** Describe business systems -> Build a database -> Plan a solution -> Automate with code
**Outcome alignment:** CLO VII, VIII, IX, XII (CLO XIII lab thread: Excel, Airtable, VBA)
**Bloom's focus:** Apply, Analyze, Evaluate, Create

### Chapter 9: How Organizations Turn Data into Decisions

**Subtitle:** DESCRIBE how information flows through a business
**Outcome(s):** CLO XII (primary), CLO VIII, IX
**Outline section(s):** VI.A (IS role in business), VIII (uses of
technology)

**Content:**

* A functional view of an organization: who needs to know what
* Management levels and the information each one runs on
* From transactions to strategy: processing systems, management
  reporting, and decision support
* Dashboards and AI agents: the new front end of business data
* Knowledge work and expert systems, then and now
* Careers reshaped by AI in every department, from marketing to
  operations to IT

**Prior-course parallel:** old module 10 (Information Systems)
**Prerequisite bridge:** the coffee-shop and bookstore scenarios from
earlier labs scale up to whole organizations.
**Skills Lab 9 (Excel):** Sort, Filter, Pivot: Answers from Sales
Data. Analyze the coffee shop's sales table and report what the pivot
reveals.

### Chapter 10: Where Data Lives: Tables, Records, and Relationships

**Subtitle:** BUILD a database that answers business questions
**Outcome(s):** CLO XIII (primary for databases), CLO VI (data
security)
**Outline section(s):** II.B.3 (databases), IX.D (database tools,
taught in Airtable)

**Content:**

* Physical versus logical views of data
* Characters, fields, records, tables, and relationships
* Key fields: how tables link without duplication
* Batch versus real-time processing
* Database types and the systems that manage them
* Data security and strategic use: every AI tool sits on top of a
  database
* Airtable in practice: bases, tables, fields, and views

**Prior-course parallel:** old module 11 (Databases)
**Prerequisite bridge:** the pivot-table questions from Chapter 9
become database queries.
**Skills Lab 10 (Airtable):** From CSV to Connected Base. Import the
pack CSVs and design the tables behind the event venue's bookings.

### Chapter 11: From Idea to Rollout: Planning Technology Solutions

**Subtitle:** OUTLINE a technology solution phase by phase
**Outcome(s):** CLO VIII, IX (primary)
**Outline section(s):** VI (planning and implementing solutions)

**Content:**

* The six phases of the systems life cycle
* Needs analysis: naming the problem before buying the fix
* Feasibility, alternatives, and build versus buy versus subscribe
* Design, acquisition, and testing
* Conversion strategies: switching systems without losing the people
* Maintenance, audits, and continuous improvement
* Prototyping, agile habits, and low-code and AI-assisted builders

**Prior-course parallel:** old module 12 (Systems Analysis and Design)
**Prerequisite bridge:** applies the business context from Chapter 9
and the database from Chapter 10 to a full project.
**Skills Lab 11 (Airtable):** Track the Project: Views, Filters, and
Reports. Run a systems proposal in a project tracker base.

### Chapter 12: Automate Your Work: Macros, VBA, and AI Coding Assistants

**Subtitle:** CREATE your first macros and explain the code behind
them
**Outcome(s):** CLO VII (primary), CLO IV, IX
**Outline section(s):** V (VBA), IX.C.5 (Excel automation)

**Content:**

* What a program is, and the six steps that produce one
* Design tools: pseudocode, flowcharts, and logic structures
* Testing and debugging: syntax errors versus logic errors
* From machine code to natural language: how programming languages
  evolved toward the prompt
* Macros: recording, running, and deciding what deserves automation
* The Visual Basic Editor: procedures, variables, and reading code you
  did not write
* AI coding assistants: drafting code fast, verifying it like a
  professional
* Where web scripting fits (connects back to Chapter 2's HTML)

**Prior-course parallel:** old module 13 (Programming and Languages)
plus the Excel macros unit the old build placed in module 9
**Prerequisite bridge:** automates the Excel skills built in
Chapters 6-9.
**Skills Lab 12 (Excel with VBA):** Record It, Read It, Rewrite It:
Your First Macro. Record a macro, then edit and explain it in the
Visual Basic Editor.

**Part IV milestone:** students can follow data from a transaction to
a decision, build and query an Airtable base, outline a systems
project, and automate a repetitive Excel task with a macro they can
read and defend.

A 16-week section adds a project week after Part III and a buffer week
before finals. Exam count and placement stay course-configured in
Canvas: the old build ran three summative exams plus a comprehensive
final, and that pattern still fits.

**Assessment structure (adapted from the Spring 2026 syllabus):**

* Orientation activities: 2%
* Discussions: 17%
* Chapter reading and Quick Checks: 20%
* Skills Labs (12): 30%
* Unit exams: 21%
* Comprehensive final: 10%

Weights carry forward from the previous build's proportions and may
shift when the Canvas shell is rebuilt.

## Cross-Chapter Dependencies

**Strict prerequisites:**

* Chapter 6 requires Chapter 5 (storage extends the memory discussion)
* Chapter 8 requires Chapter 7 (threats map onto network paths)
* Chapter 10 requires Chapter 9 (database questions come from business
  information needs)
* Chapter 12 requires Chapters 6-9 (VBA automates the Excel skills the
  lab thread built)

**Lab thread order (strict):**

* Word (Ch 1-3) -> PowerPoint (Ch 4-5) -> Excel (Ch 6-9) ->
  Airtable (Ch 10-11) -> Excel macros with VBA (Ch 12)

**Knowledge assumed across all chapters:**

* Everyday phone, app, and web experience (nothing more)
* Earlier chapters of this book only
